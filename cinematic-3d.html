01The gap

Most buyers inspect a used car for under four minutes. The person selling it has had it for years.

That asymmetry is the entire game. You're not trying to become a mechanic. You're trying to find the one reason to walk away before you've handed over the money.

A single missed head-gasket, frame repair, or rolled-back odometer costs more than this guide does — by a factor of about a thousand.

01·5What it hides
Stop 01 — Approach

Stand back first.

Before you touch anything, look at the car as one object. Does it sit level? Do the panel gaps line up? A car that's been hit hard rarely goes back together straight.

Walk away if: panel gaps don't match side to side.
Stop 02 — Body & paint

Read the paint in daylight.

Sight down each panel. Overspray, a slightly different shade, or orange-peel texture on one door means a repaint — and a repaint means a story the seller hasn't told you.

Walk away if: fresh undercoat hides "rust-free."
Stop 03 — Tires & brakes

The tires confess.

Uneven wear means alignment, suspension, or frame. Tires from four different weeks on the same car means corners were cut everywhere else too.

Walk away if: inner-edge wear plus vague steering.
Stop 04 — Under the hood

Open it cold.

A creamy film under the oil cap is coolant where it shouldn't be. A spotless engine on a high-mileage car is usually hiding a fresh leak someone just scrubbed off.

Walk away if: mayonnaise under the oil cap.
Stop 05 — Interior & VIN

Match the story.

Worn pedals and a polished steering wheel on a "low-mileage" car don't agree. Check every VIN against the title before the conversation turns to price.

Walk away if: the VIN plate looks disturbed.
Stop 06 — The test drive

Drive it like you'll own it.

Cold start, windows down, radio off. Listen. Brake hard once on an empty road. Let go of the wheel briefly on a straight. The car will tell you everything if you stop talking.

Walk away if: it pulls, shudders, or knocks under load.

06Before you ask

Reasonable questions.

Do I need any tools or experience?
No. The whole guide is built around what you can check with your eyes, your hands, a phone flashlight, and a short test drive. No jack, no scanner, no mechanical background assumed.

The checkpoints are universal — they're about physics and how cars wear, not a particular brand. Document and title specifics vary by region; the guide explains what to verify so you can apply it locally.
